## Highlights This Week

### 1. mRNA Vaccines and CD8+ T Cell Priming
**"mRNA vaccines engage unconventional pathways in CD8+ T cell priming"** (Nature, Apr 15)
Jo et al. reveal that mRNA-LNP vaccines activate T cells through unconventional antigen processing and MHC presentation pathways, distinct from traditional vaccine mechanisms. This has major implications for next-generation vaccine design.
DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10353-6

### 2. TOX as a Dual-Fate Regulator
**"The dual role of TOX in regulating TH1 and CD8+ T cell fate"** (Nature Immunology, Apr 13)
Kye & Kuchroo demonstrate that the transcription factor TOX plays opposing roles in TH1 versus CD8+ T cell differentiation, adding nuance to our understanding of T cell lineage commitment.
DOI: 10.1038/s41590-026-02494-7

### 3. Diet and CD8+ T Cell Ferroptosis
**"Diet-induced metabolic memory shapes CD8+ T cell ferroptosis susceptibility"** (Nature Immunology, Apr 13)
Dietary history imprints metabolic memory on CD8+ T cells, altering their susceptibility to ferroptosis (iron-dependent cell death). Suggests nutritional status directly impacts anti-tumor T cell longevity.
DOI: 10.1038/s41590-026-02493-8

### 4. Purine Salvage Pathway Protects CD8+ T Cells
**"Purine salvage pathway protects CD8+ T cells from metabolic stress"** (Nature Immunology, Apr 13)
The purine salvage pathway emerges as a critical metabolic safeguard for CD8+ T cells under stress conditions, potentially exploitable for enhancing adoptive T cell therapies.
DOI: 10.1038/s41590-026-02491-w

### 5. Phosphatidylserine as a Novel Exhaustion Signal
**"Exposed phosphatidylserine is an inhibitory molecule in T cell exhaustion"** (Nature, Mar 25)
Medina et al. identify exposed phosphatidylserine (PS) on the cell surface as a previously unrecognized inhibitory signal in CD8+ T cell exhaustion — expanding the exhaustion paradigm beyond classical protein receptors (PD-1, TIM-3, LAG-3).
DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10266-4

### 6. Haem-Driven T Cell Exhaustion
**"Haem from stressed mitochondria can drive T cell exhaustion"** (Nature Reviews Immunology, Apr 7)
Mitochondrial stress releases haem, which directly promotes T cell exhaustion programs. Links mitochondrial health to immune dysfunction in chronic infection and cancer.
DOI: 10.1038/s41577-026-01301-6
